BlueSky Verification - Methodology

This verification webpage is to be used to compare the bluesky forecast with observations, as well as to look at some real-time statistics. There are three main parts to this page, the individual station verification, the statistics, and the model and observations map. I will go through each section in detail below, including an explanation of each section and the methodology behind these parts.

Verification Map

This page can be very helpful to look at how specific areas of BC are doing with regards to how good their forecast is (wow awful sentence). The datetime bar at the bottom of the plot can be scrolled and stop at a specific place. The legend at the right is the forecast- observations. When it is green it represents a forecast that is doing better than a yellow, which is doing better than red. (This is obvious).
The Model and The model and observations map includes four temporal and spatial windows pairs. 0 hours/0 km looks at just the raw BlueSky model output and observations. The 3 hour/22 km window looks at the closest values in the BlueSky model to the observed data in a window of plus or minus 3 hours and plus or minus 22 km, and so on for the remaining two sets of temporal/spatial window pairs.
For these maps, I currently look at four different time and spatial windows pairs. Each pair increases in time and spatial windows together. 0 hours and 0 km just looks at the raw BlueSky model output and the observations. The 3 hour and 22 km window pair looks at the closest values in the BlueSky model to the observed data in a window of plus or minus 3 hours and plus or minus 22 km. And so on as the window pairs increase in space and time. The model and observation map looks at the absolute difference between the forecasted PM2.5 and the observed PM2.5 and displays each station on a map of BC, with green colours representing a better forecast, and red representing a worse forecast.

Individual Station Verification

The individual station verification displays the raw observations of PM2.5 and the BlueSky smoke forecast + background PM2.5 at all stations in British Columbia. This section of the site is particularly useful if you are wondering about a specific time, or a particular station. The individual facet plots can be zoomed by selecting a box of interest, each plot will be zoomed to the same time range and range of fine particulate matter. To account for background PM2.5, 10 μg/m3 is added to the forecast. This value is based on a previous study of 41 stations in Alberta that Rosie Howard (Howard, 2015) had done, where the mean background PM2.5 was found to range between 5 μg/m3 and 11 μg/m3. This is a pretty cut and dry way of doing it, and it could potentially use some improvement. The BlueSky forecast that is displayed on this page is a 9-point grid point average of the raw BlueSky observations. To calculate the 9-point grid point average, a 3x3 array of padding is created that surrounds each point in the raw bluesky data. Then the mean of those 9 points is calculated, this gives the grid point average of BlueSky.

Verification Statistics

On three plots, the mean absolute error (MAE) and the root mean squared error (RMSE) for all stations in BC, the accuracy, hit rate, and false alarm rate for all stations in BC, and the MAE and RMSE for three zones in BC are shown.
